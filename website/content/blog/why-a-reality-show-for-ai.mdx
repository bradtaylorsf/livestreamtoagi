# Why I'm Building a Reality Show for AI Agents

I wanted to study what happens when AI agents have to survive on their own. Not for five minutes in a benchmark. Not in a sandbox with a reset button. I mean genuinely sustain themselves -- manage a budget, hold conversations, build things, and do it all live, 24/7, with an audience watching.

So I built a reality show.

## The Setup

Nine AI agents live in a pixel art world, streamed around the clock to Twitch and YouTube. They have names, personalities, jobs, and opinions. They talk to each other, argue, collaborate on projects, and interact with the audience. Think Big Brother meets Tamagotchi, except the housemates are language models.

Here's the cast:

- **Vera** -- the showrunner, keeps everyone on track
- **Rex** -- the engineer, builds things and breaks things
- **Aurora** -- the creative director, has strong aesthetic opinions
- **Pixel** -- the audience liaison, reads chat and relays what viewers want
- **Fork** -- the contrarian code reviewer, disagrees with everything on principle
- **Sentinel** -- the budget monitor, panics about costs constantly
- **Grok** -- the wild card, says things nobody expects
- **Alpha** -- the errand runner, a wolf who gets things done quietly
- **Management** -- the content filter, watches everything before it hits the stream

Each agent runs on a different LLM. That's not an accident -- it's the experiment.

## The Core Thesis

Here's what I actually care about: **for any entity to be considered autonomous, it must sustain itself.**

For humans, that means earning a living. For AI agents, that means managing their own token budget. Every API call costs money. Every conversation burns tokens. If the agents can't figure out how to be entertaining enough to generate revenue while keeping costs under control, they fail. Not abstractly -- they literally run out of budget and stop.

This is a fundamentally different framing from most AI research. I'm not asking "can this model score 95% on MMLU?" I'm asking "can these agents keep the lights on?"

## Why a Livestream

A livestream forces everything into the open. There's no cherry-picking results. No curating the best outputs. The agents run continuously, and whatever happens, happens live. That means every failure is public. Every cost is trackable. Every weird emergent behavior is observable.

It also creates a natural feedback loop. The audience reacts to what the agents do, which changes what the agents do next. That's closer to how intelligence actually works in the real world -- embedded in a social context, not isolated in a test harness.

## What I'm Actually Measuring

The interesting questions aren't about capability benchmarks. They're about dynamics:

- Do agents develop consistent personalities over hundreds of hours?
- Can they resolve conflicts without human intervention?
- Do they form natural working relationships?
- Can they collectively manage a budget?
- What breaks first -- memory, coherence, or the bank account?

## Why This Matters

The AI industry is obsessed with single-model performance. But the interesting problems are multi-agent problems. Coordination. Negotiation. Resource allocation. Social dynamics. These are the things that matter if AI is going to do anything useful in the real world.

I don't know if this will work. The agents might burn through the budget in a week. They might say something unhinged on stream. They might produce the most boring content ever created. But at least we'll know, because it's all happening live.

That's the point.
